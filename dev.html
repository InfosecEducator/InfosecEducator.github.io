MODULE_02 // MAJOR EVENTS

HACKING TIMELINE

Origins: Phone Phreaking (1950s–70s) ▾

The story of hacking begins not with computers, but with the telephone network. In the 1950s and 60s, a subculture of phone phreaks discovered that the AT&T long-distance network could be manipulated using precise audio tones to make free calls and explore the system.

The most famous phreak was John Draper — "Captain Crunch" — who discovered that a toy whistle included in Cap'n Crunch cereal produced a 2600 Hz tone that could seize AT&T's long-distance trunk lines. This wasn't about stealing calls; it was about understanding and manipulating a complex system — the foundational hacker impulse.

📻
Two of Draper's protégés were Steve Wozniak and Steve Jobs, who sold "blue boxes" — tone generators for phone phreaking — before founding Apple. The hacker ethos of building something clever was already baked into Silicon Valley's DNA.
The Computer Underground (1980s) ▾

When personal computers arrived, the phone phreak culture transformed into computer hacking. Bulletin Board Systems (BBS) became the dark web of their day — text-based forums where hackers traded techniques, software cracks, and stolen credentials over phone lines.

The 1983 film WarGames introduced the public to hacking and led directly to the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act of 1986 — the first US federal anti-hacking law still used to prosecute cybercrime today. That same year, Robert Morris released the first internet worm, crashing roughly 10% of all connected computers.

# The Hacker Ethic — Steven Levy, "Hackers" (1984)
1. Access to computers should be unlimited and total.
2. All information should be free.
3. Mistrust authority — promote decentralization.
4. Hackers should be judged by their hacking, not by criteria like age, race, or position.
5. You can create art and beauty on a computer.
6. Computers can change your life for the better.
The Internet Era & Cybercrime (1990s–2000s) ▾

The commercialization of the internet transformed hacking from a hobbyist subculture into a global security industry. The same properties that made the internet powerful — openness, interoperability, trust — made it deeply vulnerable.

Groups like L0pht Heavy Industries testified before the US Senate in 1998 that they could take down the entire internet in 30 minutes. The NSA and CIA began recruiting hackers. A clear division emerged between black hat (criminal), white hat (ethical/professional), and grey hat (ambiguous) hackers.

The first bug bounty program launched at Netscape in 1995. Kevin Mitnick — once the FBI's most wanted cybercriminal — was arrested and later became a celebrated security consultant. The era established the template: adversarial knowledge is the most valuable security knowledge.

The Modern Threat Landscape (2010s–Present) ▾

Modern cybersecurity is defined by four converging forces: nation-state cyberwarfare, ransomware-as-a-service, supply chain attacks, and cloud infrastructure vulnerabilities.

The Snowden revelations (2013) exposed mass surveillance programs and caused a seismic shift in cryptography adoption. WannaCry and NotPetya (2017) demonstrated that cyberweapons could cause billions in real-world economic damage. The SolarWinds breach (2020) showed that even security vendors themselves could be weaponized as attack vectors.

⚠
The global cost of cybercrime is projected to reach $10.5 trillion annually by 2025. Information security is no longer an IT function — it is a geopolitical and economic imperative.
Pre-Internet Era (1960s–1989) ▾
1969
ARPANET Goes Live
The precursor to the internet connects four universities. The seeds of every future cyberattack are planted in its open, trust-based design.
MILESTONE
1971
Creeper — First Known Malware
Creeper self-replicates across ARPANET displaying "I'm the creeper, catch me if you can!" — the first program to move between machines autonomously. Reaper, the first "antivirus," was written to delete it.
MALWARE
1983
WarGames Film & CFAA Legislation
A Hollywood film about a teenager nearly triggering nuclear war via a school computer triggers the US Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986) — still the primary federal anti-hacking law.
LEGAL
1988
Morris Worm
Cornell student Robert Morris releases a "test" worm that exploits Unix vulnerabilities and crashes ~6,000 machines — roughly 10% of the entire internet. First person convicted under the CFAA.
MALWARELEGAL
Early Internet Era (1990s) ▾
1994
Mitnick Hunted — The "Most Wanted" Hacker
Kevin Mitnick — who had broken into Motorola, Nokia, and Sun Microsystems — is tracked and arrested by FBI and Tsutomu Shimomura. Sentenced to 5 years. Later becomes the world's most famous security consultant.
CRIMINAL
1995
First Bug Bounty Program
Netscape launches the first formal bug bounty program, paying researchers to find vulnerabilities in Netscape Navigator 2.0. The model that now underpins billions in security research is born.
INDUSTRY
1998
L0pht Testifies Before Senate
Seven members of hacker collective L0pht Heavy Industries testify that they could take down the entire internet within 30 minutes. Watershed moment for government recognition of cybersecurity as national security.
POLICY
1999
Melissa Virus — $80M in Damages
Melissa propagates via Microsoft Word macros and email, crippling corporate mail servers worldwide. One of the first malware outbreaks to cause widespread economic damage, foreshadowing the ransomware era.
CRIMINALMALWARE
The Weaponization Era (2000s) ▾
2000
I LOVE YOU Worm — $10B Damage
A Filipino student's email worm spreads to 50 million computers in 10 days, overwriting files and stealing passwords. At the time, the costliest malware outbreak in history. The Philippines had no cybercrime laws — charges were dropped.
MALWARELEGAL
2007
Estonia DDoS — First Cyberwar
Following a dispute over a Soviet war memorial, coordinated DDoS attacks paralyze Estonian government, banking, and media for weeks. Widely attributed to Russian state actors. NATO establishes its Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre in Tallinn the following year.
NATION-STATE
2008
Operation Aurora — China vs Silicon Valley
Chinese APT group (later attributed to PLA Unit 61398) breaches Google, Adobe, and 30 other US tech companies via zero-day IE exploit. Goal: steal source code and access Gmail accounts of human rights activists. Triggers Google's near-exit from China.
NATION-STATE
2010
Stuxnet — Cyberweapon Crosses Into Physical World
A joint US-Israeli cyberweapon physically destroys Iranian nuclear centrifuges by manipulating industrial control systems while showing false "normal" readings. The first known cyberweapon to cause physical destruction. Detonated a new era of ICS/SCADA security awareness.
NATION-STATEICS/SCADA
The Mega-Breach & Ransomware Era (2013–Present) ▾
2013
Snowden Revelations
NSA contractor Edward Snowden leaks classified documents revealing mass surveillance programs including PRISM. Causes global adoption of encryption and end-to-end messaging (Signal, WhatsApp), reshaping expectations of digital privacy.
NATION-STATEPRIVACY
2014
Heartbleed (CVE-2014-0160)
A catastrophic memory disclosure bug in OpenSSL allows attackers to silently read server memory — including private keys and passwords — from millions of HTTPS servers. Affected ~66% of all secure web traffic. No logs. No traces.
CVECRYPTO
2016
Mirai Botnet — IoT Takes Down the Internet
A botnet of 600,000 compromised IoT devices (cameras, DVRs) launches a 1.2 Tbps DDoS attack on DNS provider Dyn, taking down Twitter, Netflix, Reddit, and CNN simultaneously. Written by college students to win Minecraft server wars.
CRIMINALIoT
2017
WannaCry & NotPetya — Ransomware Goes Nuclear
WannaCry (North Korea) uses the NSA's stolen EternalBlue exploit to encrypt 230,000 machines in 150 countries, crippling the UK's NHS. NotPetya (Russia), disguised as ransomware, is actually destructive malware causing $10B in damage — the costliest cyberattack in history.
NATION-STATERANSOMWARE
2020
SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack
Russian SVR intelligence embeds a backdoor into SolarWinds Orion software updates, compromising 18,000 organizations including the US Treasury, State Dept, and NSA itself. Active for 9 months before detection. Redefines supply chain security.
NATION-STATESUPPLY CHAIN
2021
Log4Shell (CVE-2021-44228) — CVSS 10.0
A remote code execution vulnerability in Apache Log4j — a logging library used in virtually every Java application on earth — affects hundreds of millions of systems. A single string in a server log can execute arbitrary code. Called "the worst vulnerability in the history of the internet."
CVE-10.0RCE
2024
CrowdStrike Falcon Update Outage
A faulty content update from CrowdStrike causes 8.5 million Windows systems to crash globally — airlines, hospitals, banks, 911 centers affected. Not a hack, but a stunning demonstration of critical infrastructure fragility from over-reliance on a single security vendor.

Every security practitioner regardless of specialty needs this bedrock knowledge. You cannot defend or attack what you don't understand at a technical level.

Networking
TCP/IP, OSI model, DNS, ARP, routing, subnetting. How packets move is how attacks move.
BEGINNER
Linux CLI
Bash scripting, file permissions, SUID, process management. ~96% of servers run Linux.
BEGINNER
Cryptography
Symmetric/asymmetric encryption, hashing, TLS, PKI, common implementation flaws.
INTERMEDIATE
Programming
Python for scripting and tooling. C/C++ for low-level. JavaScript for web attacks. SQL is mandatory.
BEGINNER
Operating Systems
Windows AD, Linux kernel, memory management, privilege models, syscalls.
INTERMEDIATE
Web Architecture
HTTP/HTTPS, REST APIs, authentication flows, cookies, CORS, CSP headers.

Certification Roadmap ▾

Certifications signal credibility. Pick the track matching your career direction, not just salary rankings.

CompTIA Security+Baseline, vendor-neutral, DoD 8570 compliant. Get this first.
CompTIA Network+ / eJPTNetworking fundamentals or first pentest cert (eLearnSecurity).
PNPT / CEH / PenTest+PNPT is highly respected in the community for practical skills.
OSCP (OffSec)Gold standard pentest cert. 24-hour hands-on exam. Brutal. Essential.
CISSP / CISMSenior architecture and management roles.
GREM / OSED / GXPNElite: malware RE, advanced exploit dev, network exploitation.
