PORT OF SAN DIEGO

San Diego Unified Port District

The San Diego Unified Port District is a California public agency established in 1962 by the State Legislature to manage the tidelands and navigable waters of San Diego Bay. It serves five member cities, balancing maritime commerce, public recreation, environmental stewardship, and economic development across approximately 34 miles of waterfront. As a piece of critical national infrastructure, the Port sits at the intersection of military logistics, global shipping, and civilian commerce — making it a high-value target in the modern cyber threat landscape.

Who They Are

  • ⚓ Board of Port CommissionersSeven commissioners — one appointed by each of the five cities plus two more from San Diego. Serve staggered 4-year terms. No taxing authority; self-funded through tenant leases.
  • § Enabling LegislationCreated under the California Harbors & Navigation Code. Tidelands held in public trust — managed for commerce, navigation, and public recreation.

Responsibilities

  • 🚢 Maritime CommerceOperates commercial cargo terminals handling dry bulk, liquid bulk, break-bulk, and auto imports. Manages channel dredging and harbor navigation for deep-draft vessels.
  • 🛳 Cruise IndustryHomeport and turnaround port for major cruise lines. Manages the B Street Pier and Broadway Pier cruise terminals with 100+ ship calls annually.
  • 🏖 Public Parks & RecreationMaintains 20+ miles of public waterfront parks, bike paths, fishing piers, and open space. All tidelands are held in public trust for free public access.
  • 🌿 Environmental StewardshipClean Bay Plan, kelp restoration, eelgrass mitigation, air quality programs. Manages vessel waste, stormwater runoff, and ecological restoration across the bay.
  • 🏨 Real Estate & Tidelands LeasingLeases tidelands for hotels, restaurants, marinas, and commercial uses. Lease revenue is the primary funding source for port operations — no property taxes collected.
  • 🔒 Harbor Safety & SecurityPort police patrol the bay, terminals, and public areas. Coordinates with U.S. Coast Guard and CBP on vessel inspections, MTSA compliance, and incident response.

// incident timeline — 2018

The Ransomware Attack

Pre-2018
Normal Operations
Port IT systems manage public records, permit processing, park services, and internal operations across the waterfront. No known major cyber incidents on record.
Sep 2018
Ransomware Deployed — Systems Encrypted
Attackers gain access to Port IT infrastructure and deploy ransomware. Files across affected systems are encrypted. Port staff discover the intrusion and begin initial containment procedures.
Sep 25
Public Disclosure — Bitcoin Ransom Demanded
Port of San Diego publicly announces the attack. Attackers demand payment in Bitcoin cryptocurrency. The Port declines to confirm whether it paid. IT services are declared disrupted.
Sep–Oct
FBI & DHS Engaged — Paper Fallback Activated
Port coordinates with the FBI and DHS (including Coast Guard Cyber Command) on investigation and recovery. Affected departments revert to manual, paper-based processes for continuity.
Oct 2018
IT Services Partially Restored
Port IT begins phased restoration. Public-facing services — permit requests, public records, some administrative functions — remain degraded for several weeks. Investigation continues.
Post-2018
Security Hardening & Framework Adoption
Port invests in cybersecurity infrastructure, staff training, and incident response planning. Broader maritime sector begins adopting NIST CSF and IMO 2021 cybersecurity guidelines as direct response to the wave of port attacks.
2021+
IMO Cyber Risk Mandate Takes Effect
International Maritime Organization (IMO) Resolution MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3 requires all ships and port facilities to integrate cyber risk management into safety management systems — a direct outcome of attacks like the Port of San Diego incident.

Impact & Response

The attack targeted administrative IT systems, not operational technology (OT). Cargo movement and safety operations continued, but the disruption exposed how deeply critical infrastructure depends on connected IT networks.

// threat context & lessons learned

Why Ports Are Targets

Critical Infrastructure Ports are designated U.S. critical infrastructure under the DHS framework. They connect global supply chains, support military logistics, and handle billions in trade. Disruption has cascading economic effects — making ransomware ROI high for attackers.
IT / OT Convergence Modern ports blend traditional IT (admin networks) with Operational Technology — crane controls, vessel traffic systems, cargo management platforms. As these converge, vulnerabilities in one domain can threaten the other.
Legacy Systems Port infrastructure often runs decades-old OT systems not designed with network security in mind. Patching cycles are slow, vendor support may be expired, and air-gapping is increasingly impractical as connectivity demands grow.
Global Precedent Port of San Diego was not alone — the Port of Barcelona suffered a near-simultaneous attack in 2018. NotPetya devastated shipping giant Maersk in 2017 ($300M+ loss). The Port of Houston was targeted in 2021. Maritime cyber incidents are accelerating.
Human Attack Surface Ports employ large, diverse workforces with varied cyber hygiene — vendors, contractors, seasonal staff, crew. Phishing and social engineering remain primary initial access vectors. A single click can propagate ransomware across a flat network.
Lessons & Frameworks Post-2018 recommendations: NIST CSF adoption, network segmentation (IT vs OT), offline backups, staff phishing training, incident response playbooks, and coordination with CISA Maritime Division and Coast Guard Cyber Command.
